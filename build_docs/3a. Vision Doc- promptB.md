# NyayaSetu — Problem / Vision Doc

---

## The Problem

India has a justice gap. Not a law gap.

The laws exist. BNS, BNSS, and BSA — the three foundational statutes that govern crime, procedure, and evidence across the world's most populous democracy — are comprehensive, modern, and enforceable. But they are written for the legal system, not for the people the legal system is supposed to serve.

The result is a structural exclusion that operates silently at scale. A woman who has been harassed at her workplace does not know which section of BNS applies. A man whose car was damaged by a reckless driver does not know whether to approach the police, a magistrate, or a consumer court. A family whose land was fraudulently transferred does not know what evidence BSA requires them to preserve. They are not ignorant — they are unequipped. And the system offers them no bridge.

---

## The Scale of the Gap

- 50 million new legal cases are filed in India every year
- 50 million additional cases sit pending in Indian courts — a backlog that grows annually
- A basic legal consultation costs ₹2,000–₹10,000 — inaccessible to the 800 million Indians living on under ₹10,000/month
- In 2023–24, India replaced IPC, CrPC, and the Indian Evidence Act with BNS, BNSS, and BSA — creating a knowledge vacuum that even practicing lawyers are still navigating
- The majority of legally actionable incidents in India go unreported — not because citizens lack rights, but because they lack the knowledge and structured support to exercise them

---

## Why This Moment Is Different

The transition to BNS, BNSS, and BSA is not a routine legislative update. It is a wholesale replacement of India's three most foundational criminal laws — every section number changed, every procedural reference updated, every evidentiary standard rewritten. Citizens who once had partial familiarity with "Section 302 IPC" now face a completely new numbering system under BNS. Lawyers who have practiced for decades are recalibrating their mental maps.

This creates a 2–3 year window — before institutional knowledge catches up, before incumbents respond, before legal awareness percolates through the population — where a purpose-built intelligence layer over the new laws can establish itself as the definitive entry point to the Indian justice system. That window is open right now.

---

## The People This Affects

**Primary — The Citizen in Crisis**

Any Indian adult who has experienced a legal situation and does not know what to do next. This is not a niche demographic. It is 400 million legally active adults who interact with the justice system annually across road accidents, property disputes, workplace harassment, consumer fraud, domestic violence, cyber crime, and hundreds of other incident categories. They range from urban professionals to semi-urban small business owners to rural farmers. What they share is a common barrier: the gap between knowing something wrong happened and knowing what to do about it.

**Secondary — The Evaluator Lawyer**

A verified legal professional on the NyayaSetu platform who reviews high-stakes or low-confidence cases escalated by the agentic pipeline. This role validates the system's output in edge cases, maintains platform integrity, and creates a human safety net for situations where AI reasoning alone is insufficient.

**Tertiary — Law Firms and Institutions**

Law firms that currently spend 2–4 hours on unstructured client intake per case. Legal aid NGOs that serve underserved populations with too few lawyers. Law schools that need real-world legal reasoning tools for training. Police stations that receive poorly documented complaints that waste investigative resources. All of these institutions benefit when citizens arrive informed, documented, and legally oriented.

---

## The Vision

NyayaSetu — Sanskrit for "bridge to justice" — is the digital infrastructure layer between every Indian citizen and every legal action they will ever need to take.

Not a chatbot. Not a search engine. Not a lawyer marketplace.

A legal intelligence platform: a citizen describes what happened to them in plain language — the way they would describe it to a trusted friend — and the system returns a structured, expert-grade legal response. Which laws apply. Which clauses are relevant. What the exact statutory text says. What it means in plain language. And, for those who need to act, a structured document — FIR-ready or lawyer-ready — that they can walk into a police station or law firm with immediately.

The long-term vision is larger than a product. NyayaSetu becomes the standard through which legal actions are initiated in India. Every police station has a NyayaSetu QR code. Every law firm receives pre-structured intake via NyayaSetu case IDs. Every legal aid NGO uses NyayaSetu to serve more citizens with fewer lawyers. And eventually — every common-law democracy with a justice gap has access to the same infrastructure.
